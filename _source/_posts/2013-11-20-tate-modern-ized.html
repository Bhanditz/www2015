Eighteen years ago, I stood in line at Tate Gallery to see Damien Hirst's Mother and Child, Divided. Hirst, the Turner Prize winner that year, and l'enfant terrible of British Art, had surgically halved bodies of a cow and her calf and immersed them in massive vitrines of formaldehyde. That was my first introduction to Tate.

It would be another six years before Creative Commons would be born, and a dozen years before the CC0 Public Domain Dedication would be invented. Last week Tate announced it would release its metadata under CC0 so all may benefit from that information. It seems opportunistic to say this, but I am glad my life has been touched by these great institutions.

Tate sets a very high bar for art, for pushing the boundaries of what we can imagine, even for what we might find acceptable, forever challenging, always breathtaking, and never complacent. CC leverages the existing copyright regime and provides us with way more flexibility than the default copyright license allowing us to share our creations with others on our own terms. Going even further, CC0 sets the highest bar possible for opting out of IP protection completely, removing all conceivable legal hurdles to reuse. We applaud Tate for releasing the metadata under CC0 so it may be used for scholarly and other purposes.

Though I can't imagine many artists to be directly interested in this data, but imagine I am an art scholar and I want to know how many male artists born after 1980 and still alive have their works at Tate. So, I downloaded the data from Tate's Github page, and wrote a little script that allows me to answer such questions.

Works, but it is of limited use. The code is provided below. Since this is mostly throwaway code, I won't discuss it further here, but I do think there are ways to make such data even more useful. More on that in a different post.

  1. #!/opt/local/bin/perl
  2.  
  3. use 5.10.1;
  4. use strict;
  5. use Text::CSV;
  6.  
  7. my $dir = 'collection';
  8.  
  9. unless (@ARGV) {
  10.  say "Enter key value pairs for match. For example, you can enter:\n\n" .
  11.   "\tperl tate.pl gender male born 1980 died no\n\n" .
  12.   "to find all make artists born after 1980 and still alive.\n" .
  13.   "Keys are id, name, gender, born, died, place_born, place_died, url";
  14.  exit;
  15. }
  16.  
  17. my %args = @ARGV;
  18.  
  19. find_artist(\%args);
  20.  
  21. sub find_artist {
  22.  my ($args) = @_;
  23.  my $csv = Text::CSV->new({ binary => 1 }) or
  24.     die "Cannot use CSV: " . Text::CSV->error_diag();
  25.  
  26.  my @artists = ();
  27.  my ($c_gender, $c_born, $c_died) = (0, 0, 0);
  28.  
  29.  open my $fh, "<:encoding(utf8)", "collection/artist_data.csv" or die $!;
  30.  while (my $row = $csv->getline( $fh )) {
  31.   if (lc($row->[2]) eq lc $args->{gender}) {
  32.    $c_gender++;
  33.    if (($row->[4] ne '') && ($row->[4] >= $args->{born})) {
  34.     $c_born++;
  35.     if ($args->{died} eq 'no') {     
  36.      if ($row->[5] eq '') {
  37.       $c_died++ && ($row->[9] = 'no');
  38.       push @artists, $row;
  39.      }
  40.     }
  41.     else {
  42.      if (($row->[5] ne '') && ($row->[5] <= $args->{died})) {
  43.       $c_died++ && ($row->[9] = "in or before $args->{died}");
  44.       push @artists, $row;
  45.      }
  46.     }
  47.    }
  48.   }
  49.  }
  50.  $csv->eof or $csv->error_diag();
  51.  close $fh;
  52.  
  53.  say "found $c_gender " . lc($args->{gender}) . " artists\n" .
  54.   "of whom $c_born were born in or after $args->{born}\n" .
  55.   "of whom $c_died " .
  56.   ($args->{died} eq 'no' ? ($c_died == 1 ? 'is' : 'are') .
  57.   " still alive\n" : "died in $args->{died}\n");
  58.  
  59.  say "$_->[2] artist $_->[1] born in or after $_->[4] died $_->[9]" for @artists;
  60. }
