Since 2004, Creative Commons has maintained an active presence in the sciences. We believe that our vision - of an internet that reaches its full potential through open systems - must extend to scientific and technical research.

To support scientific benefits via the commons, we work in three core projects: increased access to the scholarly record via Open Access, supporting the emerging Open Data movement, and research and development into how the Creative Commons methodology can be applied beyond the copyright. These three projects echo our mission of developing, supporting, and stewarding legal and technical infrastructure that maximizes creativity, sharing, and innovation.

Open Data
Science@CC explores the use of Creative Commons copyright licenses and other legal tools on scientific data, ranging from database rights to privacy. We work to increase Open Data through adoption of our licenses and tools in large open scientific projects, with successful implementations in taxonomic, energy, genomics, disease research, geospatial, polar, and bilbiometric disciplines. We also provide expert guidance on Open Data to funders, institutions, private foundations, governments, the corporate sector, and other stakeholders.

R&D
Science@CC has developed and deployed CC-inspired legal tools for transferring biological materials, and maintains an active research project into the potential roles that standardized licensing and open data might play in the patent system. Our early adopters include the Personal Genome Project, with 1,000 lines of stem cells licensed under a liberal Creative Commons Materials Transfer Agreement, CHDI's repository of Huntingtons Disease research tools, and the GreenXchange project.

Video on the Biological Materials Transfer Project.

We also support a wide variety of users interested in the sciences and the digital commons, including libraries, research funders, publishers, and technology transfer professionals.
