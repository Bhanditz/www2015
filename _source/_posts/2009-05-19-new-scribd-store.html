Scribd.com is the popular self-described "social publishing company" that allows anyone to upload and share their documents, whether it's a report, a musical score, or the next great Russian novel. Scribd has long had CC licensing built in, which allows you to choose under what conditions you would like to share your work, similar to Lulu.com. Before the Kindle, there was Scribd and its iPaper viewer, which, though not as fancy, was and is still completely free.

Now Scribd has gone a step further with the new Scribd store. From the NYTimes,

"In the new Scribd store, authors or publishers will be able to set their own price for their work and keep 80 percent of the revenue. They can also decide whether to encode their documents with security software that will prevent their texts from being downloaded or freely copied.

Authors can choose to publish their documents in unprotected PDFs, which would make them readable on the Amazon Kindle and most other mobile devices. Scribd also says it is readying an application for the iPhone from Apple and will introduce it next month."

An alternative to Amazon prices, Scribd e-books can go for as little as $2 a pop, which still gives authors $1.60 per book, more than what they would normally be getting under a traditional publishing contract.

However, security software seems kind of extreme, akin to what the LA Times describes as an "iTunes" approach to books. iTunes is great for downloading individual songs, but you also can't share that song with someone in a different part of the world, unless they've got your password.
